### LinkedIn (Wed 2026-05-06) — "Closing the loop on locally processed meat"

**Image:** Wide of the farm, captioned with subtitle on slide.

**Post (≈ 180 words):**

For two years our regeneratively raised beef and lamb had to leave Mangaroa Farms to be cut. The animals were raised here. The pasture, the genetics, the grazing rotation — all in the valley. But the butchery was outsourced.

Next weekend that closes. Rob is opening our on-farm butchery, and we're returning to a model where the same hands move the animal from paddock to cabinet. Three benefits we're watching for:

— **Mauri kept intact.** Less transport stress, shorter chain of custody, no "lost in translation" between paddock and plate.

— **Lower carbon footprint per kilogram.** Eliminating the off-farm processing leg removes a significant transport and energy cost.

— **Closer relationship with the eater.** When Rob can talk you through a cut at the cabinet, the food has a story you can hold.

Locally raised. Locally processed. We'll share what we learn over the months ahead.

— The team at Mangaroa Farms

### LinkedIn (Wed 2026-05-20) — "Halter virtual fencing in regen practice"

**Post (≈ 220 words):**

A short note on adaptive grazing in practice.

We use Halter virtual fencing on our Angus herd at Mangaroa. Each animal wears a collar. Paddock boundaries are drawn on a phone, not strung in wire. The herd moves on a schedule we can change overnight without rebuilding fence lines.

What this enables, and what we're seeing in year two:

— **Tighter mob, longer rest.** We can graze a small area hard for 24 hours and then move on, leaving the paddock to recover for 60-90 days. Soil structure is rebuilding faster than under set-stocking.

— **Genuine adaptive grazing.** When a paddock is wet, we can pull the herd off in 20 minutes from a phone, not a half-day rebuild.

— **Less pugging, less compaction.** We're seeing earthworm counts climb in the rotation paddocks compared to baseline.

It's not a silver bullet. Halter has trade-offs — collar weight, battery life, learning curve for the herd. We're three seasons in and still learning. But the alignment between virtual fencing and regen principles is real, and we wanted to share what we're seeing.

Happy to compare notes with anyone running similar systems.

— The team at Mangaroa Farms

### LinkedIn (Wed 2026-06-03) — "Whole-animal economics for regen producers"

**Post (≈ 200 words):**

Sharing some pricing thinking from our first month of locally processed sales at Mangaroa.

Cut-and-wrapped retail is the sustainable foundation — but whole-animal orders are where the regen-economics story actually closes.

A side of beef sold whole brings the per-kilogram price down for the eater (≈ $10-13/kg vs. $25-35/kg in retail cuts) **and** raises the per-animal margin for the farm. Less waste, less labour, less freezer churn.

We're noticing that customers who buy a side of beef tend to:
— ask more questions about how the animal was raised
— come back for the next animal three months later
— bring a friend in on the next order

It's a different relationship to food. Closer to how it worked when most New Zealanders had a freezer share with a neighbouring farmer.

We don't think it scales infinitely. We do think it's a structural answer to the question of how to make regen meat *actually affordable* for households who care, while keeping the farm viable.

If you're a regen producer thinking about this — happy to share what we've learned about cut-list templates, customer education, and the freezer-storage objection. DM open.

— The team at Mangaroa Farms
