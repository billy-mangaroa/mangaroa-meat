# Bringing It Home: How Mangaroa Farms Relocalised Its Meat

*Draft article for On the Land magazine — 850 words*

---

There is a moment in the life of every farming operation where a decision has to be made about how close to the final product you want to get. For most, the answer has been to hand off at the farm gate and let someone else handle the rest. At Mangaroa Farms in the Hutt Valley, they have chosen a different path.

The farm, a regenerative operation set in Mangaroa Valley just outside Upper Hutt, has begun processing its own beef and lamb. After professional slaughter by Preston's, the carcasses come home. From there, Rob and the crew handle all the butchery and processing on site — turning whole animals into the cuts that end up in the farm shop cabinet and in customers' kitchens.

It is a move that has been a long time coming. Rob, who manages the farm, is also the person doing the butchery. The same hands that manage the pastures and move the cattle prepare the cuts. It is hard to get closer to the full story than that.

"We have always told a provenance story," says the team at Mangaroa. "But there was a gap between the animal on our paddock and the product in the shop. Bringing the butchery in-house closes that gap."

The farm runs regenerative practices across its beef and lamb operation — using virtual fencing to manage grazing rotations, cover crops to build soil, and systems that produce more with fewer inputs. The Holter cattle graze on open paddocks alongside diverse pasture, and the team takes a whole-systems approach to land management that improves the land year on year.

For the small but growing number of New Zealanders who care about where their food comes from, this kind of transparency matters. Every cut in the Mangaroa shop can now be traced back to a specific paddock and a specific animal raised on the farm. The supply chain is as short as it gets — from the pasture to the butchery to the counter.

**A practical, not ideological, decision**

The move to local processing was driven by practicality as much as principle. Shorter supply chains mean fresher product. Doing the processing on site means more control over quality, timing, and the range of cuts offered.

"It is not about doing everything ourselves for the sake of it," the team explains. "It is about doing it properly and having more care at every step."

The professional slaughter is still handled externally — that part of the process requires specialist facilities and expertise. What has changed is everything that happens after. The butchery, the cutting, the packing, the presentation — that is now local.

**What is on offer**

The shop carries standard retail cuts of beef and lamb: steaks, mince, sausages, chops, roasts, and more. The range changes week to week depending on what has been processed. There is also an online ordering option for customers who want to browse and pick up from the farm shop.

For those with bigger appetites — or bigger freezers — the farm takes expressions of interest for whole lamb, sides of beef, and custom bulk orders. It is a model that appeals to families, food co-ops, and anyone who wants to stock up on quality meat they can trust.

**Regenerative from the ground up**

The meat is only part of the story. Mangaroa Farms has been building a regenerative farming system for years — one that treats the land not as a resource to extract from, but as a living system to invest in.

That means virtual fencing for precise grazing management, cover crops for soil biology, native planting for biodiversity, and a commitment to working with natural systems rather than against them. The farm describes it as "growing food in harmony with nature" — a phrase that captures the regenerative philosophy without the jargon. The farm is also home to a community food hub, an events programme, and an education centre that teaches regenerative practices to the next generation.

The meat operation fits into this bigger picture. It is not a standalone product line — it is an extension of a farming philosophy that values connection to land, community, and food.

**A model worth watching**

In a country that exports the vast majority of its meat, the idea of a farm selling its own locally processed product direct to the community is still unusual. But it is exactly the kind of short, transparent supply chain that consumers increasingly want.

Mangaroa is not trying to scale to nationwide distribution. The ambition is local — to provide the community with quality meat from a farm they can visit, from animals they can see, processed by people they know.

Fresh beef and lamb are available at the Mangaroa Farms shop at 108 Whitemans Valley Road, Upper Hutt, and online at meat.mangaroa.org.
